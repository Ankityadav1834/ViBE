\section{Experiment 3: SEI Model Comparison}
\label{sec:exp3}

\subsection{Objective}

Compare all seven SEI growth models under identical electrochemical conditions to identify mechanistic differences in capacity fade and SEI thickness evolution. This is Claim 3 of the thesis.

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{itemize}
  \item 50 CC-CV cycles at 1C
  \item All other parameters identical (Chen2020)
  \item Metrics: $L_\text{SEI}(t)$, capacity fade $\Delta Q$, $R_\text{SEI}(t)$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Expected Model Ranking by Aggressiveness}

Based on the mathematical formulations:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textit{Tunnelling}: most aggressive at early cycles (thin SEI), most self-limiting later
  \item \textit{Reaction-limited}: moderate, temperature-sensitive
  \item \textit{Solvent-diffusion limited} (Chen2020): parabolic growth, standard reference
  \item \textit{EC-reaction limited}: transitions from reaction-limited to diffusion-limited
  \item \textit{Interstitial-diffusion limited}: OCP-dependent, slow at high anode potential
  \item \textit{Electron-migration limited}: very slow at typical $U_n$
  \item \textit{Constant}: zero growth (baseline)
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experiment 4: 5S5P Heterogeneity Propagation}
\label{sec:exp4}

\subsection{Objective}

Quantify how a single heterogeneous branch (weaker cooling + higher contact resistance) affects current redistribution, thermal divergence, and differential SEI growth in a 25-cell pack. This is Claim 2 of the thesis.

\subsection{Setup}

Refer to \cref{sec:5s5p} for the heterogeneity parameters. The simulation runs for 100 CC-CV cycles at 2C ($I_\text{pack} = 50$~A).

\subsection{Key Metrics}

\begin{description}
  \item[Current redistribution:] The heterogeneous branch (higher $R_\text{contact}$) carries $\sim 15$--$20\%$ less current than nominal branches.
  \item[Temperature divergence:] The heterogeneous branch (lower $hA$) reaches $\sim 5$--$8\si{\celsius}$ higher temperature despite lower current.
  \item[SEI spread:] After 100 cycles, the heterogeneous branch accumulates $\sim 2\times$ more SEI thickness.
  \item[Capacity imbalance:] The heterogeneous branch has higher SOC at end of discharge due to reduced current uptake.
\end{description}

\subsection{Physics Interpretation}

The thermal--electrochemical feedback creates a self-reinforcing degradation loop:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Higher $R_\text{contact}$ $\to$ less current in branch $p=2$
  \item Lower current $\to$ less Joule heating \emph{from current}, but lower $hA$ $\to$ higher $T$
  \item Higher $T$ $\to$ faster SEI reaction-limited growth (Arrhenius factor $\propto e^{E_a/\mathcal{R}T}$)
  \item More SEI $\to$ higher $R_\text{SEI}$ $\to$ further reduces current uptake
\end{enumerate}
This coupling is only accessible through full DFN-level simulation.

\section{Experiment 5: Balancing Strategy Comparison}
\label{sec:exp5}

\subsection{Objective}

Evaluate passive, active-capacitor, and active-inductor balancing against the no-balancing baseline in the 5S5P heterogeneous pack, using full electrochemical simulation. This is Claim 4.

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Delta V_\text{pack}$ spread at end of charge
  \item $\Delta L_\text{SEI}$ spread after 50 cycles
  \item Energy efficiency (balancing current $\times$ time)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Expected Findings}

Based on the literature~\cite{Daowd2011,Baughman2008} and the physics of the system:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Passive balancing reduces voltage spread most effectively during CV phase but wastes energy.
  \item Active inductor balancing achieves the most uniform SOC at end of charge.
  \item All balancing strategies reduce but do not eliminate differential SEI growth, because the thermal heterogeneity persists.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiment 7: Exact Local Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:exp7}

\subsection{Objective}

Demonstrate the exact local sensitivity analysis capability using reverse-mode automatic differentiation (PyTorch autograd) to compute the SOC-dependent sensitivity of temperature rise rate ($\dot{T}$) to physical parameters. This demonstrates Contribution C8.

\subsection{Setup}

A single cell is subjected to a 1C constant-current discharge without pre-aging. At predefined SOC checkpoints (90\%, 70\%, 50\%, 30\%, 10\%), the simulation state is frozen, and a single backward pass is executed through the discretized PDE residual to simultaneously compute the exact gradients of the pack-averaged temperature rise rate with respect to five physical parameters: solid diffusivity ($D_{s,n}$), anode reaction rate ($m_{\text{ref},n}$), electrolyte porosity ($\varepsilon_{e,n}$), contact resistance ($R_{\text{contact}}$), and cooling coefficient ($hA$).

The normalized sensitivity is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
    S_i = \frac{\partial(dT/dt)}{\partial p_i} \cdot \frac{p_i}{|dT/dt|}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Results and Physical Interpretation}

The autograd-based evaluation computes the exact derivatives in less than 20 seconds, bypassing the $2N_p+1$ requirement of traditional finite difference perturbation loops.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cooling coefficient ($hA$)}: Dominates at low SOC (e.g., $S_{hA} \approx -19.3$ at 30\% SOC). As the cell warms during mid-discharge, heat removal governs the thermal balance.
    \item \textbf{Contact resistance ($R_{\text{contact}}$)}: The largest positive driver, rising sharply towards the end of discharge ($S \approx +5.6$ at 30\% SOC) due to pronounced ohmic heating.
    \item \textbf{Anode reaction rate ($m_{\text{ref},n}$)}: Increases in magnitude as SOC drops, reflecting that lower exchange current densities produce higher overpotentials and therefore more irreversible heat.
    \item \textbf{Solid diffusivity ($D_{s,n}$)}: Shows zero instantaneous sensitivity to temperature rise rate, correctly reflecting that solid diffusion influences the internal concentration profile rather than the instantaneous thermal balance at a frozen state.
\end{itemize}

These results prove that the differentiable tensor architecture successfully scales to extract exact local sensitivities from the coupled multi-physics DFN system.

\section{Solver Performance}
\label{sec:solver_perf}

\subsection{Newton Convergence}

Under normal operating conditions (1C--2C), the Newton solver converges in 2--4 iterations per timestep. At high C-rates ($>3C$) or during the onset of lithium plating, 6--8 iterations may be required. Failure to converge triggers a timestep halving ($\Delta t \leftarrow \Delta t / 2$).

\subsection{Adaptive Timestep Behaviour}

The \texttt{AdvancedSolver} (LTE control) achieves:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Large timesteps ($\Delta t \sim 50$--$150$~s) during quasi-steady mid-discharge
  \item Automatic refinement ($\Delta t \sim 1$--$5$~s) during fast dynamics (CV phase, beginning/end of discharge)
  \item $\sim 3$--$5\times$ fewer total function evaluations than fixed-step \texttt{BasicSolver} for equivalent accuracy
\end{itemize}

\subsection{GPU Scaling}

For a GPU-capable machine, the \texttt{vmap} batching provides near-linear scaling with pack size because:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Jacobian evaluations are parallelised across all cells
  \item The batched linear solve (\texttt{torch.linalg.solve}) uses CUDA batched LU
  \item The per-cell state size ($N_\text{state} \approx 40$--$50$) fits comfortably in GPU registers
\end{itemize}

Expected GPU speedup (relative to sequential CPU): $5$--$20\times$ for packs of $N_\text{cells} \geq 10$.
